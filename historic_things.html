Lion Throne

Sihasana or Thihathana Palin
The Lion Throen, one of eight histroic thrones used by Burmese monarchs, was saved from destruction during World War II by being transported to Indiea in 1885 after the Third Anglo-Burmese War. Make of yamane wood,it feactures small lion figurines inside niches and is known as the Thihathana Throne or Royal Lion Throne.

The upper portion of the throne, called "U-gin", feaures eleven Chakra and Devas on both sides.The throne is believed to represent the Solar and Lunar dynasties,with two guardian gods (Lokanat) on each side of the door. 33 Nats (Devas) are installed at the U-gin, the door and door jamb of the throne. At the left and right sides of the throne, there are Lokanat figures, a flying lion, and a flying elephant as symbols of peace. The Lion Throne was used for judicial affairs at the Supreme Court of the Myanansankyaw Golden Palace in the Yadanabon period.

Protohistoric Period

Miniature models of pagodas at Bagan, Myanmar

Small scale models and architectural elements of UNESCO's World Heritage listed Halin, Beikthano and Sri Ksetra Pyu cities and other early Myanmar city states from all over the country are on show in this room. Moreover, five Pyu figurines, as well as gold, silver, bronze and earthen artifacts from excavated sites are displayed in this exhibition room.

Prehistoric Period

Replica of temple at Bagan, Myanmar

In this exhibition room, tools, weapons and utensils from Myanmar Stone Age, Bronze Age and Iron Age are displayed. Miniature replicas of Padah-Lin Caves are displayed with supporting special lighting and models. The center piece of the display is a miniature replica of the Bronze Age excavation site, with supporting objects, such as pots, urns, beads and bronze weapons.
